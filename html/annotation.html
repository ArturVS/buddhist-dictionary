Text Management and Chinese Text Reader for the Chinese Buddhist Canon

This page gives an introduction on the methodology used to analyze the Chinese Buddhist canon, as presented on this web site. The Chinese text reader for the Taishō shinshū daizōkyō 《大正新脩大藏經》 Buddhist canon was generated using a freely available tool cnreader. The HTML pages generated were uploaded to this web site to be available to web users. The source of the texts is the CBETA web site, which distributes the texts freely under a Creative Commons license. The NTI Reader Project is very thankful to CEBTA for this wonderful contribution to the general public and subsequently makes the content of the NTI Reader freely available under an English version of the same license.

Background

The collection of texts in this web site are managed as a text corpus. A text corpus, or simply corpus, is a term used in linguistics to mean a representative collection of texts used to study the linguistic characteristics of a language. The Brown Corpus, first published in 1961 [Francis 1964], was the first large corpus developed. It consists of about 1 million English words. The corpus of Chinese texts from the Taishō used on this site has 59,069,881 words with 33,200 unique words, and 73,414,250 characters, according the summary of the Corpus Analysis.

Each corpus entry includes meta data about a primary source document, such as the source and links to plain text versions, English translations, vocabulary analysis, and other information.

Gloss is a form of annotation that explains the meanings of words in texts. A collection of glosses is called a glossary. A historic example is Biblical gloss that was used to annotate the meanings of words in the Bible. Biblical gloss was usually added in margins and included commentaries as well. Interlinear gloss is gloss added between the lines. Historic annotations are valuable in themselves and may give explainations of the source text that would not be known otherwise. However, they have often been ommitted when documents were new versions of documents were published, retaining the original text only.

Gloss is also used in a modern context by linguists to help them study and discuss languages that they cannot understand themselves. Typically, this kind of gloss in added after the native terms in square brackets, such as 佛 [Buddha]. Besides the meaning of foreign words in English, linguists also sometimes indicate pronunciation with gloss. The Penn Chinese Treebank has a particular style of gloss used that includes gloss for indivual words in square brackets and angle brackets for the phrase as a whole [Nianwen 2005]. For example, 乘 [riding] 白 [white] 象 [elephant] <riding a white elephant>. This web site follows that style with a mouse-over for the convenience of readers.

Corpus management

Each corpus entry has a text file that uses a markdown file. Markdown is a plain Markdown can allow people who are not software or web design specialists to easily work with plain text that can be transformed to HTML later. Markdown consists of simple and intuitive patterns, such has headers and bullet lists for making plan text more readable. This makes it easy to add annotations to primary source documents or copies of them.

The collection of primary texts and markdown documents is through a set of tab separated variable files.

Word Frequency Analysis

After documents have been tagged they can be used to compile bigram and unigram word frequencies, which helps with word sense disambiguation. This is discussed more on the page Word Frequency Analysis on this site and in the book Natural Language Processing with Python by Steven Bird, Ewan Klein and Edward Loper.
